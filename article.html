Where a Child Lives

Where a child is born shapes their odds of being stunted almost as much as how poor their family is.

Nehchal Singh·2026·8 min

Stunting, being more than two standard deviations below the World Health Organization's median height for a child's age, is one of the most consequential measures in development. It is the body keeping score of everything that went wrong early: too little food, too much illness, too little of the conditions a child needs to grow. And what it costs is not recovered. Stunted children carry the deficit forward into weaker cognitive and physical development, lower productivity, and worse health across their whole lives.WHO

So a stunting map is not really a map of height. It is a map of which childhoods a place protects and which it does not. India's tells a strange story when you read it closely: prosperity predicts the broad pattern but misses the most interesting states, the penalty of being born poor swings from almost nothing to nearly fifty points depending on where you are, and even the richest children are not reliably spared. Four cuts of the same survey, one conclusion.

01The outcome

The figures here come from the National Family Health Survey, round five (NFHS-5), the most recent nationwide round available and the standard source for these numbers; it was collected across 2019 to 2021, so the picture is as current as the data allows. In it, the national stunting rate sits around a third of all children under five. Getting to that number cleanly takes some care, and the choices matter. Each child's height-for-age score carries special survey codes for measurements that were missing or never taken, so those were set aside rather than treated as real values, which would otherwise have dragged the average toward nonsense. A second filter removed biologically impossible scores, anything beyond six standard deviations from the reference median, since a child that far out almost always reflects a recording error rather than a real child. And every child was weighted by the survey's own sampling weights, so that groups the survey deliberately oversampled do not distort the national picture. With those three steps, the rate lands almost exactly on the official published figure, which is the reassurance that the pipeline is sound.

The map shows how little that single national number tells you. The spread is more than twofold. At the low end, Tamil Nadu and Sikkim sit near 20 to 22 percent, with Kerala close behind at 23. At the high end, Meghalaya reaches 47 percent, Bihar 43, and Jharkhand and Uttar Pradesh both 40. A child in Meghalaya is more than twice as likely to be stunted as a child in Tamil Nadu. Same country, same survey, same year, and a difference that large.

What stands out is that the map does not divide neatly into rich and poor regions. Gujarat, among India's wealthier states, records 39 percent, higher than far poorer states. The far south and the small northeastern states like Sikkim do well; the central and eastern belt does badly. Geography, not just income, is written across this map.

A child in Meghalaya is more than twice as likely to be stunted as one in Tamil Nadu, in the same country and the same year.

02Income explains part of it, but Gujarat breaks the rule

If prosperity were the whole answer, plotting each state's economic output against its stunting rate would give a tidy downward line: richer states, fewer stunted children. The next chart does broadly slope that way, but the scatter around the line is the real story.

A word on what that line is, because it is easy to over-read. It is an ordinary least-squares fit across roughly thirty states, drawn purely as a descriptive benchmark, not a causal estimate. It answers one modest question, namely how stunting tends to move with income on average, so that we can see which states sit above or below that tendency. It is deliberately not a claim that raising income by some amount would lower stunting by some amount, and with only about thirty points the line is a loose summary that a couple of outliers can tilt. Read it as a reference line, nothing more.

Bihar sits exactly where you would expect, very low income and very high stunting, near the top-left corner. The interesting states are the ones far off the line. Gujarat is the clearest case: a relatively prosperous state with output per person well above the middle of the pack, yet carrying 39 percent stunting, sitting high above the line, doing far worse than its economy would predict. Punjab tells a milder version of the same story. Below the line sit the over-performers, states getting more nutrition out of their economic hand than expected: Kerala, Manipur, and Puducherry all land there.

The income measure itself needs an honest label. The horizontal axis is state output per person, not household income. Households do not earn a state's domestic product, and the figure is distorted for small states with concentrated economies: Goa and Sikkim look extraordinarily rich because a small population divides an outsized output, which inflates the number without making the average family wealthy. So their low stunting at the far right should be read with that caveat in mind, and the axis is named for output rather than income to keep the claim honest. The lesson is not that income does not matter, since it clearly sets a tendency. It is that the tendency is loose enough to leave Gujarat and Kerala, at similar prosperity, on opposite sides of the line. Something other than money is doing the work.

03Within states, poverty bites very unevenly

The next cut goes inside each state, comparing the stunting rate of the poorest fifth of households with the richest fifth. If poverty were uniformly punishing, this gap would be roughly constant. It is not. It ranges from essentially nothing to nearly fifty percentage points.

Here the definition of rich and poor is itself a deliberate choice. The survey does not use reported income, which is notoriously unreliable, especially where work is informal and seasonal. Instead it builds a wealth score from what a household actually owns and the quality of its housing and sanitation, then ranks every household across all of India into five equal groups. The poorest and richest fifths used here are the bottom and top of that national ranking. This asset-based measure captures long-run living standards better than a single year's income would, which is exactly why it tracks child nutrition more faithfully.

Delhi has the widest gap of all, 48 percentage points between its poorest and richest children. Haryana follows at 36, then a cluster of large states, Bihar and Jharkhand at 29, Punjab and Andhra Pradesh at 28, where being born poor is a heavy penalty on a child's growth. In these places the rich and poor live in effectively different nutritional worlds.

At the other extreme, Puducherry's gap comes out slightly negative, with its richest children marginally more stunted than its poorest. But this is the point where the data asks for restraint rather than excitement. Puducherry has one of the lowest overall stunting rates in the country, so both its poorest and richest fifths are small numbers, and the difference between two small, noisily measured rates is mostly noise. The negative sign is interesting, not meaningful: it sits well within the range where a slightly different sample would push it back to zero or above. What it does not show is rich children being genuinely worse off. Sikkim and Chandigarh, sitting near zero, are best read the same way.

04And it isn't only about poverty

The final map isolates only the richest fifth of households in each state, children whose families are, by national standards, well-off, and asks how many of them are still stunted. If stunting were purely about poverty, this map would be pale almost everywhere. It is not.

Among the richest fifth, stunting still runs from about 7 percent up to 32. Tripura's wealthiest children are stunted at 32 percent, higher than the overall rate of several entire states. Maharashtra's richest fifth sits at 28, and Gujarat's, Delhi's, and Uttar Pradesh's at 25. These are not poor households. They are the top of their state's wealth distribution, and a quarter or more of their children are still too short for their age.

Only a handful of places get their richest children near the floor: Meghalaya at 7 percent, Sikkim at 10, Odisha at 11. Notice that Meghalaya, which had the worst overall rate at 47 percent, has the best outcome for its richest children. That single contrast carries the whole argument. In Meghalaya, wealth buys a child out of stunting almost entirely; in Tripura or Maharashtra, it does not. The protective power of money itself depends on where you are. It also resolves the puzzle from the previous map: a narrow rich-poor gap can hide the fact that a state is failing children up and down the ladder, not just at the bottom.

Tripura's richest children are stunted at 32 percent, higher than the overall rate of states like Tamil Nadu or Kerala.

05What the four maps say together

Read in sequence, the data tells one coherent story. Stunting is widespread and spread unevenly, from 20 to 47 percent across states. Economic output explains part of that unevenness but leaves clear exceptions: prosperous Gujarat does worse than poorer states, while Kerala does better than its income predicts. The penalty of poverty within a state ranges from nothing in Puducherry to 48 points in Delhi. And even the richest children are stunted at rates between 7 and 32 percent, with the protective value of wealth varying enormously by place.

The common thread is place. The state a child is born in, its sanitation, its health systems, its institutions, its diets, shapes their odds of stunting nearly as much as their family's wealth, and sometimes more. A rich child in Tripura faces worse odds than a poor child in parts of the south.

This is a description, not a diagnosis, and it is worth being strict about the difference. Every figure here shows an association, not a cause. A state that does better than its income predicts may be doing something right, or may simply differ in ways this data does not capture, and the maps cannot tell you which. Throughout, the safer choices were made on purpose: weighting the sample, discarding impossible measurements, using assets rather than reported income, and labelling the trend line as descriptive rather than causal. None of that turns correlation into explanation. What the data does establish, clearly and repeatedly, is that the question worth asking is not only how poor a child's family is. It is where that child lives.
