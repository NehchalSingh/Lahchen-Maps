The Five Indias of Women's Status

State averages hide as much as they reveal.I let the data sort 707 districts on their own with a simple machine learning exercise, and the "two Indias" quietly became five, with development and women's status refusing to travel together.

In a widely shared monologue, Vir Das described two Indias living inside the same country- one of progress, one of constraint, somehow sharing an address. It landed because most of us have felt it: opportunity and limitation sitting side by side, often on the same street.

Conversations about women in India tend to run along the same lines. Haryana carries a reputation for restrictive norms and thin female agency; Kerala, Goa, and parts of the Northeast get cast as gentler places to be a woman. These shorthand maps are useful but they flatten a much messier reality.

Here's a simple hunch worth testing: if women's status really varies across India, the variation shouldn't stop at state borders. A "bad" state should still hold pockets of relative progress; a celebrated one should still hide districts that struggle. State averages, however tidy, can bury all of that local texture.

So instead of starting from regional stereotypes, I handed the question to the data. Using India's National Family Health Survey (NFHS-5), I built a district-level portrait of women's status from four simple ingredients (variables) — female education, household wealth, access to information (newspaper readership), and gender norms and let an algorithm group 707 districts by how they actually look, not by which state they sit in.

The result isn't two Indias. It's five. And the more interesting part is what the grouping exposes: development and gender norms don't always move together. Some districts are comparatively prosperous yet socially conservative; others are poor but more progressive. Before any of the method, here's the map those five groups make.

Once the map is viewed through regional rather than state boundaries, the anomalies begin to glow. Prosperous, progressive districts emerge inside states known for conservatism, while conservative enclaves linger inside places celebrated for social progress.

Read this before the colours

One variable needs a rather careful interpretation. Three of the four: education, wealth, newspaper reading are solid, full-sample measures. Gender norms is not. It comes from a set of survey questions about domestic violence (whether a woman rejects every listed justification for a husband hitting his wife), asked of a smaller sub-sample and carrying its own separate weighting.

So treat the norms axis as relative, not absolute. We can say one cluster rejects those justifications more often than another but we should not read it as a precise thermometer of how equal a place is. Norms is one of four ingredients here, but not the headline. The story is the five Indias, and the colours of the map mean "this combination of development and attitudes," not "good place / bad place."

Method How these five groups were actually built ›

The short version. Four district-level variables: education, wealth, newspaper readership, and gender norms (the norms caveat above applies throughout; it's one ingredient, not the headline). Each is standardised so no single variable dominates by sheer scale, then reduced with principal component analysis: the first component (54.5% of the variation) is essentially "development" as it includes education, wealth and newspaper moving partially together; the second (25.8%) is norms, almost entirely on its own. The districts are then grouped into five clusters based on these components.

Three clusters provide the cleanest statistical separation, but they largely reduce India to a simple development hierarchy. Four and five clusters perform almost identically on the silhouette metric, suggesting little statistical difference between them. The five-cluster solution is retained because it separates out a small but distinctive Development Frontier that would otherwise be absorbed into a broader developed group. The loss in statistical fit is negligible, while the additional cluster reveals one of the most interesting patterns in the data.

Development Frontier 23 districts · 3.3% of all districts · the smallest group

A small group of districts that rank highest on the combined measures used in this study: female education, household wealth, access to information, and gender norms. They record the strongest outcomes for women overall and form a distinct cluster at the top of the distribution. Their lead is pronounced on education, wealth, and information access, while there is a smalldisadvantage on norms which may be because of small or inadequate sample size in the chosen districts. This also reflects the fact that attitudes toward wife-beating capture only one dimension of gender norms, or that differences become harder to distinguish among districts already near the top of the scale.

Comfortably above average on development and on rejecting violence against women. The combination most people imagine "doing well" looks like and it shows up in some surprising states.

Still developing economically, yet relatively more progressive on gender norms, more so than their schooling and wealth would predict. Proof that attitudes can run different from core variables.

Patriarchal Core 125 districts · 17.7% · developed, yet traditional on this measure

Low-Resource Progressives 227 districts · 32.1% · the largest group · the development climb

The least developed cluster, lowest on schooling, wealth, and information. Its norms, notably, sit around the middle, not at the bottom. The challenge here is opportunity, not attitudes.

02 — A fair questionWhy five groups, and not four?

An honest admission: by the usual statistical rule three clusters separate the districts a touch more cleanly than five (silhouette 0.45 vs 0.39). Four clusters don't even manage that, they score marginally below five (0.385 vs 0.389). So why five, and why not three?

Because going from four to five didn't reshuffle anything. The four big groups stayed put; the fifth simply peeled off a small, distinctive cluster: Development Frontier, those 23 most-developed districts that would have been folded into a broader "developed" group at four. Statistically it's optional. Substantively, it was too interesting to merge back: a tiny band of front-running districts is exactly the kind of pocket this whole exercise set out to find.

Method What "didn't reshuffle" means, precisely ›

Cross-tabulating the four-cluster and five-cluster solutions shows the fifth group is carved almost entirely out of one existing cluster, while the other groups map across nearly one-to-one. The internal separation score (silhouette) is highest at three clusters and declines gently as you add more. so five is a deliberate choice, disclosed choice in favour of an interesting small group, not a number the statistics handed us. Figure 4 shows this as a merge tree, built directly from the cluster centroids rather than drawn by hand: whichever cluster sits on its own branch and joins last is the one the data treats as most distinctive.

03 — The geographyWhich states fall where

This is where the opening hunch pays off. None of the five Indias is a single state and every cluster is a blend, and several land far from their stereotype.

Development Frontier

Concentrated in the coastal and metropolitan South and a few Northeastern pockets: Kerala, Karnataka, Goa, Mizoram, Tamil Nadu, Puducherry. The familiar high-development names, here distilled to their strongest districts.

Development-Norms Alignment

A prosperous north-and-west belt: Maharashtra, Punjab, Haryana, Himachal Pradesh, Delhi, Gujarat. Note the surprise: Haryana, a state with a hard reputation on women's status, Still, these clusters are based on only four indicators and should be interpreted as a simple typology of relative performance, not a complete assessment of women's status.

Intermediate Transition

Much of the central belt: Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, plus parts of Maharashtra, Gujarat and Assam. Districts that aren't wealthy, but whose attitudes on the norms question sit ahead of where their development would place them.

Patriarchal Core

The South again, but a different face of it: Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh. These are not poor districts; they simply score lowest on the wife-beating-justification question. That's a genuinely odd result, and the reason the norms caveat sits so early in this piece, the measure runs against development here in a way that's well documented and not fully understood (see the limitations below).

Low-Resource Progressives

The classic low-development core: Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand. The plains states that anchor most development gaps — though, again, their norms scores are middling rather than worst, which is its own quiet correction to the stereotype.

Put together, the picture is the one the opening predicted: the South splits into front-runners and a paradox; the Hindi belt splits into the merely-poor and the quietly-progressive; and supposedly-restrictive states turn up inside the most balanced cluster. Two Indias could never hold all of that.

04 — The patternDevelopment and norms part ways

Strip everything back and one finding survives: a district's development tells you surprisingly little about its gender norms. Plot the two against each other and they barely line up, which is the entire reason five groups exist instead of a single rich-to-poor gradient.

That said, and to keep norms in its place. this is still a statement about relative positions on one imperfect measure, not a verdict on any state's culture. The honest, useful version of the finding is modest: prosperity and women's status are loosely coupled at best, so policy that assumes "grow the economy and equality will follow" is leaning on an assumption the district data doesn't support.

05 — How it was builtThe method

The essentials, for anyone who's interested.

Method The four ingredients, exactly ›
  • Education — share of women with secondary schooling or above (v106 ≥ 2).
  • Wealth — mean household wealth index (v190, an ordinal 1–5 scale).
  • Newspaper — share reading a newspaper at least weekly (v157 ≥ 2), a proxy for information access.
  • Norms — share rejecting every listed justification for wife-beating (v744a–e all 0), from the domestic-violence sub-sample, weighted by d005.

The first three are weighted by the full-sample weight v005; norms by its own module weight, on fewer respondents which is exactly why it's treated as the soft, relative axis throughout.

Method The clustering, ANOVA, and silhouette numbers ›

Covered in full, with the actual PCA loadings, silhouette table, and ANOVA results, in the "How these five groups were actually built" drawer back in §01 — not repeated here to avoid saying it three times. Short version: standardise the four ingredients, reduce with PCA (development and norms come out as two near-independent components), cluster with k-means on those two components, and validate with ANOVA plus a development-only robustness re-run. Everything is reproduced in the accompanying notebook.

07 — The fine printWhere to be careful

Limitations, plainly

Norms is the soft spot. It rests on one question, a smaller sub-sample, and separate weighting — and on this measure India's developed South scores lowest, a long-documented and not-fully-explained reversal. Read the norms axis as relative ordering, never as a precise index of equality.

Five is a choice, not a verdict. Three clusters separate the data marginally better (silhouette 0.45 vs 0.39); four doesn't even manage that. Five is kept because it surfaces an interesting small group, and that judgement is disclosed rather than hidden — see the real numbers in §01.

Survey units vs. map units. The model groups 707 NFHS units; the map and table render administrative districts, matched by name, so coverage is high but not total — unmatched districts are shown in grey, not dropped.

Wealth is ordinal. A 1–5 category averaged across a district isn't a precise income figure. None of these break the five-India structure — but all belong on the page.
