The Five Indias of Women's Status

State averages hide as much as they reveal. Let the data sort 707 districts on their own, and "two Indias" quietly becomes five — with development and women's status refusing to travel together.

NFHS-5 IR microdatak-means · k=5~10 min read

In a much-shared monologue, Vir Das described two Indias living inside the same country — one of progress, one of constraint, somehow sharing an address. It landed because most of us have felt it: opportunity and limitation sitting side by side, often on the same street.

Conversations about women in India tend to run along the same lines. Haryana carries a reputation for restrictive norms and thin female agency; Kerala, Goa, and parts of the Northeast get cast as gentler places to be a woman. These shorthand maps are useful — and they flatten a much messier reality.

Here's a simple hunch worth testing: if women's status really varies across India, the variation shouldn't stop at state borders. A "bad" state should still hold pockets of relative progress; a celebrated one should still hide districts that struggle. State averages, however tidy, can bury all of that local texture.

So instead of starting from regional stereotypes, I handed the question to the data. Using India's National Family Health Survey (NFHS-5), I built a district-level portrait of women's status from four deliberately plain ingredients — female education, household wealth, access to information (newspaper readership), and gender norms — and let an algorithm group 707 districts by how they actually look, not by which state they sit in.

The result isn't two Indias. It's five. And the more interesting part is what the grouping exposes: development and gender norms don't always move together. Some districts are comparatively prosperous yet socially conservative; others are poor but strikingly progressive. Before any of the method, here's the map those five groups make.

Look at the South and the northern plains as two different weather systems, and the outliers start to jump out — a scatter of prosperous, progressive districts inside states you'd never expect, and conservative pockets inside states you would.

Read this before the colours

One ingredient needs a health warning. Three of the four — education, wealth, newspaper reading — are solid, full-sample measures. Gender norms is not. It comes from a single survey question (whether a woman rejects every listed justification for a husband hitting his wife), asked of a smaller sub-sample and carrying its own separate weighting.

So treat the norms axis as relative, not absolute. We can fairly say one cluster rejects those justifications more often than another; we should not read it as a precise thermometer of how equal a place is. Norms is one of four ingredients here — not the headline. The story is the five Indias, and the colours of the map mean "this combination of development and attitudes," not "good place / bad place."

01 — The castMeet the five Indias

Named for what each group actually is — leading with development, and kept deliberately fair. From the most developed to the least:

The Front-Runners 23 districts · the smallest group

The most developed districts in the country, full stop — top of the table on schooling, wealth, and information access. A small, distinctive cluster of metros and high-performing pockets where opportunity for women is widest.

Developed & Progressive prosperous and egalitarian

Comfortably above average on development and on rejecting violence against women. The combination most people imagine "doing well" looks like — and it shows up in some surprising states.

Progressive Heartland attitudes ahead of incomes

Still developing economically, yet noticeably progressive on gender norms — more so than their schooling and wealth would predict. Proof that attitudes can run ahead of the balance sheet.

The Southern Paradox developed, yet traditional on this measure

Middling-to-strong on development, but the lowest scores on the norms question of any cluster — and overwhelmingly southern. The single most counter-intuitive group, and the one where the norms caveat above matters most (more on this below).

Catching Up the development climb

The least developed cluster — lowest on schooling, wealth, and information — and the largest. Its norms, notably, sit around the middle, not at the bottom. The challenge here is opportunity, not attitudes.

02 — A fair questionWhy five groups, and not four?

An honest admission: by the usual statistical yardstick, four clusters — or even three — separate the districts a touch more cleanly than five. So why five?

Because going from four to five didn't reshuffle anything. The four big groups stayed put; the fifth simply peeled off a small, distinctive cluster — The Front-Runners, those 23 most-developed districts — that had been folded into a broader "developed" group at four. Statistically it's optional. Substantively it was too interesting to merge back: a tiny band of front-running districts is exactly the kind of pocket this whole exercise set out to find.

Method What "didn't reshuffle" means, precisely ›

Cross-tabulating the four-cluster and five-cluster solutions shows the fifth group is carved almost entirely out of one existing cluster, while the other groups map across nearly one-to-one. The internal separation score (silhouette) is highest at three clusters and declines gently as you add more — so five is a deliberate, disclosed choice in favour of an interesting small group, not a number the statistics handed us. 03 — The geographyWhich states fall where

This is where the opening hunch pays off. None of the five Indias is a single state — every cluster is a blend, and several land far from their stereotype.

The Front-Runners

Concentrated in the coastal and metropolitan South and a few Northeastern pockets — Kerala, Karnataka, Goa, Mizoram, Tamil Nadu, Puducherry. The familiar high-development names, here distilled to their strongest districts.

Developed & Progressive

A prosperous north-and-west belt — Maharashtra, Punjab, Haryana, Himachal Pradesh, Delhi, Gujarat. Note the surprise: Haryana, a state with a hard reputation on women's status, contributes districts to the most balanced cluster of all. Exactly the within-state pocket the stereotype hides.

Progressive Heartland

Much of the central belt — Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, plus parts of Maharashtra, Gujarat and Assam. Districts that aren't wealthy, but whose attitudes on the norms question sit ahead of where their development would place them.

The Southern Paradox

The South again, but a different face of it — Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh. These are not poor districts; they simply score lowest on the wife-beating-justification question. That's a genuinely odd result, and the reason the norms caveat sits so early in this piece — the measure runs against development here in a way that's well documented and not fully understood (see the limitations below).

Catching Up

The classic low-development core — Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand. The plains states that anchor most development gaps — though, again, their norms scores are middling rather than worst, which is its own quiet correction to the stereotype.

Put together, the picture is the one the opening predicted: the South splits into front-runners and a paradox; the Hindi belt splits into the merely-poor and the quietly-progressive; and supposedly-restrictive states turn up inside the most balanced cluster. Two Indias could never hold all of that.

04 — The patternDevelopment and norms part ways

Strip everything back and one finding survives: a district's development tells you surprisingly little about its gender norms. Plot the two against each other and they barely line up — which is the entire reason five groups exist instead of a single rich-to-poor gradient.

That said — and to keep norms in its place — this is a statement about relative positions on one imperfect measure, not a verdict on any state's culture. The honest, useful version of the finding is modest: prosperity and women's status are loosely coupled at best, so policy that assumes "grow the economy and equality will follow" is leaning on an assumption the district data doesn't support.

The five Indias aren't five income brackets. They're five different ways development and women's status combine — and they don't combine the way the headlines assume.

05 — How it was builtThe method, in drawers

The essentials, for anyone who wants them. Skip freely — the story above stands without opening a single one.

Method The four ingredients, exactly ›
  • Education — share of women with secondary schooling or above (v106 ≥ 2).
  • Wealth — mean household wealth index (v190, an ordinal 1–5 scale).
  • Newspaper — share reading a newspaper at least weekly (v157 ≥ 2), a proxy for information access.
  • Norms — share rejecting every listed justification for wife-beating (v744a–e all 0), from the domestic-violence sub-sample, weighted by d005.

The first three are weighted by the full-sample weight v005; norms by its own module weight, on fewer respondents — which is exactly why it's treated as the soft, relative axis throughout.

Method From 707 districts to five groups ›

Each district's four numbers are standardised (so wealth doesn't dominate by sheer scale), reduced with principal components, and grouped with k-means. The first component (~55% of the variation) is "development" — education, wealth and newspaper moving together; the second (~26%) is essentially norms on its own. The clustering runs on those two components. Cluster names are assigned afterwards from each group's own profile, so they describe the data rather than a remembered run.

Method Does the grouping hold up? ›

Two checks. An analysis of variance across the clusters is significant on all four ingredients — the groups are genuinely distinct, not visual artefacts. And re-running the grouping on the three development variables alone (dropping norms) produces a different split, which confirms norms carries real, independent information rather than echoing wealth. Both are reproduced in the accompanying pipeline.

07 — The fine printWhere to be careful

Limitations, plainly

Norms is the soft spot. It rests on one question, a smaller sub-sample, and separate weighting — and on this measure India's developed South scores lowest, a long-documented and not-fully-explained reversal. Read the norms axis as relative ordering, never as a precise index of equality.

Five is a choice, not a verdict. Three or four clusters separate the data marginally better; five is kept because it surfaces an interesting small group, and that judgement is disclosed rather than hidden.

Survey units vs. map units. The model groups 707 NFHS units; the map and table render administrative districts, matched by name, so coverage is high but not total — unmatched districts are shown in grey, not dropped.

Wealth is ordinal. A 1–5 category averaged across a district isn't a precise income figure. None of these break the five-India structure — but all belong on the page.
